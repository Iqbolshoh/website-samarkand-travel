History of Samarkand

Founded in the 8th century BC, Samarkand was the capital of the ancient state of Sogdiana, as mentioned in the Zoroastrian book "Avesta." Known as Marakanda to the Romans and Greeks, it was conquered by Alexander the Great in 329 BC. Samarkand flourished as a key stop on the Great Silk Road and became the capital of Amir Timur's empire, experiencing significant cultural and architectural growth.

In 1868, Russian forces conquered Samarkand, and it later became part of the Turkestan ASSR after the 1917 revolution. From 1925 to 1939, it served as the capital of Uzbekistan. Today, Samarkand is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, blending ancient traditions with modernity.

Landmarks and Attractions

Samarkand is renowned for its stunning medieval architecture, with landmarks that captivate visitors from around the world.

Registan Square

The heart of Samarkand, featuring three majestic madrassahs: Ulugbek (1420), Sherdor (1636), and Tillya-Kari (1660). A UNESCO World Heritage Site, this architectural ensemble is a testament to the city's historical significance.

Gur-Emir Mausoleum

The final resting place of Amir Timur and his family, built in 1404. This majestic mausoleum is a symbol of Samarkand's cultural and architectural grandeur during the Timurid dynasty.

Bibi-Khanum Mosque

Constructed in 1404 for Amir Timur's wife, this mosque features a large stone Quran platform and intricate architectural details, reflecting the city's historical splendor.

Museums of Samarkand

Samarkand's museums offer a deep dive into its rich history and cultural heritage, showcasing artifacts and stories from centuries past.

Afrosiab Museum

Located near the ancient Afrosiab site, this museum displays artifacts from the 8th century BC, including frescoes from a Samanid palace, offering insights into Samarkand's ancient history.

Ulugbek Observatory Museum

Chronicles the scientific advancements of Mirzo Ulugbek, featuring a reconstructed observatory and a unique sextant used for astronomical observations in the 15th century.
