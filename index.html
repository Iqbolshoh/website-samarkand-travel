History of Samarkand

8th Century BC

Founded as Marakanda, Samarkand becomes the capital of Sogdiana, as referenced in the Zoroastrian "Avesta."

329 BC

Alexander the Great conquers Samarkand, cementing its role as a key hub on the Great Silk Road.

14th Century

Amir Timur makes Samarkand the capital of his empire, sparking a golden era of art and architecture.

1868

Russian forces conquer Samarkand, integrating it into the Russian Empire.

1925–1939

Samarkand serves as Uzbekistan’s capital, later earning UNESCO World Heritage status for its cultural treasures.

Landmarks & Wonders

Marvel at Samarkand’s architectural masterpieces, adorned with vibrant tiles and majestic domes.

Registan Square

A UNESCO World Heritage Site, featuring the Ulugbek (1420), Sherdor (1636), and Tillya-Kari (1660) madrassahs, showcasing Timurid splendor.

Gur-Emir Mausoleum

Built in 1404, this mausoleum houses Amir Timur’s tomb, renowned for its turquoise dome and intricate mosaics.

Bibi-Khanum Mosque

Constructed in 1404 for Amir Timur’s wife, this mosque boasts a grand stone Quran platform and stunning design.

Museums of Samarkand

Explore the rich heritage of Samarkand through its museums, preserving centuries-old artifacts and stories.

Afrosiab Museum

Near the ancient Afrosiab site, this museum showcases 8th-century BC artifacts, including Samanid palace frescoes.

Ulugbek Observatory Museum

Highlights Mirzo Ulugbek’s 15th-century astronomical achievements with a reconstructed observatory and sextant.
