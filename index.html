History of Samarkand

8th Century BC

Samarkand, known as Marakanda, is founded as the capital of Sogdiana, as noted in the Zoroastrian "Avesta."

329 BC

Alexander the Great conquers Samarkand, marking its significance on the Great Silk Road.

14th Century

Amir Timur establishes Samarkand as the capital of his empire, ushering in a golden age of culture and architecture.

1868

Russian forces conquer Samarkand, integrating it into the Russian Empire.

1925–1939

Samarkand serves as the capital of Uzbekistan, later becoming a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Landmarks & Wonders

Discover Samarkand’s architectural masterpieces, adorned with intricate tilework and majestic domes.

Registan Square

The heart of Samarkand, featuring the Ulugbek (1420), Sherdor (1636), and Tillya-Kari (1660) madrassahs, a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Gur-Emir Mausoleum

Built in 1404, this mausoleum is the resting place of Amir Timur, famed for its turquoise dome and intricate mosaics.

Bibi-Khanum Mosque

Constructed in 1404 for Amir Timur’s wife, this mosque features a grand stone Quran platform and stunning architecture.

Museums of Samarkand

Delve into Samarkand’s rich heritage through its museums, preserving centuries-old artifacts and stories.

Afrosiab Museum

Located near the ancient Afrosiab site, this museum displays 8th-century BC artifacts, including Samanid palace frescoes.

Ulugbek Observatory Museum

Showcases Mirzo Ulugbek’s 15th-century astronomical achievements with a reconstructed observatory and sextant.
